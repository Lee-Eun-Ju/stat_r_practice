  1. Delays are typically temporally correlated: even once the problem that caused the initial delay has been resolved, later flights are delayed to allow earlier flights to leave. Draw a the scatter plot between mean of dep_delay of flights and the dep_delay of the immediately preceding flight. Hint 1: Using lag(), explore how the delay of a flight is related to the delay of the immediately preceding flight. Use lag() to get the info of dep_delay of a previous flight in the same origin. Before using lag(), the data should be ordered by origin, month, day, dep_time. Hint 2: group by the dep_delay of the immediately preceding flight and find the mean of dep_delay of current flights.
corr = flights %>% 
  select(origin, year, month, day, dep_time, dep_delay) %>% 
  arrange(origin,year, month, day, dep_time) %>% 
  group_by(origin) %>% 
  mutate(lag_dep_delay = lag(dep_delay))

corr %>% 
  group_by(lag_dep_delay) %>% 
  mutate(delay_mean = mean(dep_delay, na.rm=TRUE)) %>% 
  ggplot(mapping=aes(x=lag_dep_delay,y=delay_mean)) +
  geom_point(alpha=0.1) + geom_smooth()

delay = flights %>% 
  group_by(origin, dest) %>% 
  mutate(min_air_time = min(air_time, na.rm=TRUE),
         delay_in_air = air_time - min_air_time,
         sd_air = sd(air_time, na.rm=TRUE),
         standardization = delay_in_air/sd_air) %>% 
  select(air_time, origin, dest, carrier, min_air_time,
         delay_in_air, sd_air, standardization, everything()) %>%
